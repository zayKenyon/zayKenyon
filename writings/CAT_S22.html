Foreword

In this writing, the CAT S22 Flip will henceforth be known as the 'Cat'.

The writing assumes you're already tending towards a dumbphone, it does not make an argument for one. This is a piece on the Cat, not on Digital Minimalism, though there is naturally an overlap.

It's not a Dumbphone

Shock. Horror. Why bother then? Dumbphones are Feature Phones, like the Nokia Brick (old and new). Whilst the Cat is a flip phone, it runs Android 11 Go (the lite version). It is technically a smartphone but it is commonly referred to as a dumbphone because of its limited practicality.

I like to think about the Cat in two minds. Firstly, it's a transitionary phone, it's for those not yet ready or those who simply don't want to abandon modern operating systems (you're likely stuck with Android). I think it's a fair enough camp, it's a pretty big ask of somebody to uproot their entire workflow and jump straight to a Brick, especially when they have no alternatives set-up for what they currently do on their phone. The second group of people are those who can't turn away from a smartphone. I use WhatsApp for scheduling work shifts, if I took it off my phone and could only check my messages on my laptop, I would never be quick enough to pick up those shifts and then I would go hungry :/

Transitioning to a less-capable phone isn't really about "doing less" in life. Digital Minimalism isn't about scaling back and living a more isolated life, away from being able to interact with the world, it's about using technology intentionally. My current strategy is to separate 'tasks' into different categories and having different devices for those. The Cat fulfils my Communication category: Text‚úÖ, Call‚úÖ, WhatsApp‚úÖ.

Making a Plan

When my Cat arrived, it stayed in the box for a week. The plan I had made about how to use it seemed impractical once it was in my hands. For a long time now I've dumbed down my smartphones. My favourite launcher is Unlauncher on Fdroid, I run my devices in greyscale; it made the leap from a smartphone to the Cat more manageable but also more pointless. I would recommend trying a minimalist launcher as a feel for the minimalist life.

For a little while I had planned to rent a new Sim, my current (would be 'old'/'public') number would stay in the smartphone, and for all purposes, that's the number I would give out to everybody. The new sim would go in the Cat, known only by my partner with knowledge only to ring me in a true emergency. Importantly, I could still contact 999. In times where I want to be alone, it would allow me to go on my walks or cycle rides in a true disconnected-but-safe manner. When comparing compatible sims (more on this later), I found SMARTY's sims to be a solid deal, though I'm yet to see them stocked in a store. It seems that large supermarkets aren't selling such a wide collection of sims anymore. They're available on Amazon, but I can't bring myself to checkout a ¬£0.10 (pre-activation) item with a ¬£3.99 delivery fee.

On WhatsApp you can add 'Linked Devices', recent changes allow these devices to not even need a Sim card even if they're not a PC. I have the Cat as my primary device, with my tablet and laptop as linked devices. I would recommend doing something similar, texting longer messages on the Cat is arduous, and the screen is a little small to see loved ones.

Niche Points of Consideration

My smartphone used to be a major device in my two-factor authentication system. When logging into a service, I might have to open an app and approve the login from there, or I might have to wait for a notification and tap 'Confirm'; actions that aren't possible on a dumbphone, before I switched to the Cat, I was already in the midst of replacing phone auth with a security token. I wouldn't recommend putting up a stink with somebody demonstrating their persuasiveness in wanting your smartphone. Since typically leaving my smartphone at home, I feel less weight on my shoulders when walking around the city because the barrier of re-entry to my accounts is low-to-unaffected (some services still primarily use SMS auth üôÑ).
